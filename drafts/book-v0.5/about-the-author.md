## From Anhui to CERN: A Journey of Access

Wen Gong’s journey began in a rural village in Anhui, China. In a place where poverty was the baseline and the horizon was often limited to the next field, there was only one path out: education. It was a path he took with a physicist’s focus—leading from the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC) to a Ph.D. in nuclear physics in the United States, and eventually to the halls of Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory and the experimental pits of CERN in Geneva.

This origin story is not mere background; it is the "initial condition" for everything Wen builds today. 

Having seen how institutions and access can transform a life, Wen views the current state of AI with a physicist’s skepticism and an engineer’s pragmatism. Today, the power of AI is locked behind barriers of technical fluency, expensive cloud accounts, and complex programming frameworks. Wen’s mission is to dissolve those barriers—not as an act of charity, but as a "debt repayment" to the systems of education and open research that once gave him a chance.

## The Physicist’s Instinct: Declarative Thinking

Wen’s career has spanned the evolution of modern computing—from witnessing the birth of the World Wide Web at CERN to seeing the first distributed computing projects like SETI@home. But it was his training under the legacy of Werner Heisenberg at the Max-Planck-Institut that shaped his deepest technical instinct: **the declarative approach.**

In physics, you cannot always specify the "how"—the unobservable path of a particle. Instead, you formalize the "what"—the observables—and let the underlying mathematics resolve the rest. This is the core philosophy of **SPL 2.0 (Structured Prompt Language)**. Just as SQL allows a data engineer to query millions of rows without knowing the disk's rotation speed, SPL allows an AI practitioner to orchestrate complex "agentic" workflows without writing low-level Python "glue" code.

## The AI Quartet and the 48-Hour Sprint

Wen is a practitioner of the **Human × AI Partnership**. This book was produced by a four-member research team he calls the **AI Quartet**: Wen Gong (human author and architect), Claude (Anthropic's AI assistant, for technical execution and precision), Gemini (Google's AI assistant, for breadth and recent literature), and Z.ai (ZhiPu's AI assistant from China, for critical adversarial review). Each brings a different "frequency" to the work:

- **Wen** — vision, architectural intuition, and lived experience building declarative systems
- **Claude** — technical execution, code correctness, and structural precision
- **Gemini** — breadth of reference, research coverage, and editorial feedback
- **Z.ai** — adversarial review, critical assessment, and cross-cultural perspective

This book itself is a product of this collaboration. It was drafted, refined, and validated in an iterative loop that mirrors the "Conductor" metaphor: Wen provides the vision and the "score," while the AI members of the AI Quartet execute, critique, and improve the performance. It is a model of how the next generation of engineers — especially those in the Global South — can scale their impact using commodity hardware like a used GTX 1080 Ti and a declarative language like SPL.

## The Vision: AI as a Public Utility

Wen Gong believes that AI computation should be as accessible as the Web. Through **Momagrid** and **SPL 2.0**, he is building a "resilience layer" for AI—a decentralized, open-source infrastructure where a child in a rural village can "conduct their own symphony" of intelligence using a smartphone and a simple, declarative score.

*Wen Gong is currently an AI engineer, a former application developer at Oracle and cloud data engineer at Vanguard, and a lifelong builder committed to making AI a universal tool for human potential.*
